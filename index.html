About Me

I am currently a post-doctoral associate at the Department of Aerospace and Ocean Engineering (AOE), Virginia Tech. I finished my PhD also from the AOE, Virginia Tech in Spring 2016. My research interests include application of fluid-structure interaction (aeroelasticity), structural dynamics, reduced-order modeling and multidisciplinary design optimization for aerospace and mechanical systems.

Research

  • Reduced-order unsteady transonic analysis for large aspect-ratio wings:

Analysis of unsteady transonic flow is a computationally challenging process and usually approached using latest computational fluid dynamics (CFD) tools. Application of such procedure for coupled fluid-structure interaction, especially for aircraft design and optimization is thus not possible. A new approach is being developed by using indicial functions which can predict accurate unsteady airfoil behavior for attached flows. For large aspect-ratio wings the present approach can be applied via strip theory thereby reducing several order of states from the full-order three dimensional CFD model.

 

 

  • Structural and aeroservoelastic optimization of unmanned aerial vehicle exposed to strong gust and maneuver loads:

The study is investigating the proper gust load factor evaluation for unmanned drones and subsequent structural design optimization studies for the development of a versatile autonomous vehicle capable of performing multiple missions.

 

  • Truss-braced wing aeroelasticity and multidisciplinary design optimization research:

Truss-braced wings (TBW) have shown large potential for reducing fuel burn and take-off weight of commercial airplanes like Boeing 777 and Boeing 737 when studied using the multidisciplinary design optimization tools at Virginia Tech. This research explored the effect of aeroelasticity on the design of TBW in a multidisciplinary design and optimization environment.

 

The unique modal coupling of the TBW leading to flutter was explored and the its effects on the optimized designs were studied. Also, the potential of unique control effector mechanisms were evaluated which will utilize the flexibility of the TBW to enhance its aeroelastic performance by increasing flutter speed and improving roll control authority of the elastic vehicle.

 

  • Semi-active vibration control of connected buildings using recurrent neural networks:

Recurrent neural networks (RNN) can provide robust input-output mapping of dynamical systems with phase lag and which may undergo time delay between input and response. Thus, the RNN architecture was used to model the inverse dynamics of magnetorheological dampers and applied for vibration control of connected buildings using Linear Quadratic Regulator.
