Truss-braced wing aeroelasticity research

 

Multidisciplinary design and optimization studies performed by Virginia Tech in collaboration with Georgia Tech and NASA Langley Research Center have shown that truss-braced wings (TBW) carry great potential for improving fuel burn for commercial aviation. The unique configuration due to their unique synergy of structural and aerodynamic behavior leading to overall improved performance. However, the unique configuration of the truss-braced wings are associated with unique and non-intuitive aeroelastic behavior. This has triggered a multitude of investigations to properly understand  the physics behind truss-braced wing aeroelasticity.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Effect of flutter on the multidisciplinary design optimization of truss-braced wing vehicles

 

Truss-braced wing show unique and non-intuitive flutter behavior. Higher bending modes couple with torsion modes with the frequency ratio between the coupling modes close to 1. This makes such configurations prone to flutter.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 This study explored the effect of flutter on the design of TBW in a multidisciplinary design and optimization environment. Results showed that TBW configurations are prone to flutter owing to their unique geometric configuration and flutter should always be included as a constraint in the design of TBW vehicles.

 

Aeroelastic applications of a variable geometry raked wingtip

The potential of the idea that loss of control effectiveness in flexible wings can actually be used to enhance their aeroelastic performance needs proper evaluation before it can be put to use. The idea was implemented via raked wingtips which can sweep in the plane of the wing and applied on truss-braced wing (TBW) vehicles designed for Boeing 737 and Boeing 777 type of flight missions.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Results showed that when such a concept can be applied via a variable-geometry raked wingtip which can sweep in the plane of the wing, up to 10% fuel reduction is possible by either preventing flutter or improving the roll controllability of TBW vehicles.
